About Me

My curiosity has led me to explore the fields of mechatronics and robotics, where I’m excited by the possibilities of intelligent design and automation.

As I continue to grow and develop as an engineer, I invite you to take a look at some of the work I've done so far and follow along on my journey.

3D Printed Robotic Arm

(Work in Progress)

Project Details

Building a robotic arm has been a project I have wanted to pursue for a long time, and the image above shows the first iteration of its physical components. The project is currently in progress as I develop a detailed CAD model in SolidWorks, designed to achieve six degrees of freedom while housing an Arduino microcontroller and multiple servo motors. To improve reliability, the initial ball-and-socket joint will be replaced with a more standard hinge-like mechanism, ensuring greater stability and precision. Internal channels are being incorporated into the design to manage wiring and maintain a clean overall look. Since all components will be 3D printed, I am optimizing the model to minimize material usage and reduce print times without compromising structural integrity. This process is strengthening my skills in additive manufacturing and applying Design for Additive Manufacturing principles.

Laptop Stand Design

Project Details

For this project, I designed an adjustable laptop stand with the goal of improving ergonomics and usability while keeping the structure lightweight and efficient to manufacture. I created the CAD model in SolidWorks and optimized the geometry to provide strength and stability while minimizing material usage. The stand incorporates a slot and sliding mechanism that allows adjustment to different heights, ensuring flexibility of use while maintaining airflow around the laptop. I carefully defined tolerances for the sliding components to ensure smooth motion and precise fit, accounting for 3D printing constraints. Next steps include optimizing the design for my printer’s build volume, running FEA simulations to assess performance under load, and adding grooves to the slot mechanism to prevent unintentional collapse.

3D Printing Explorations – Gears, Springs, and Tools

Project Details

This project is an ongoing exploration of 3D printing as I familiarize myself with my first printer. To build a foundation, I have been creating a variety of sample parts for learning and testing purposes, which has helped me better understand tolerancing, slicer settings, and how parameters such as infill density and layer height affect print time.

One of my first test parts was a spiral spring, which I used to study the flexibility of PLA and evaluate how easily printed parts can bend. This experiment will help me optimize future spring designs. I also designed and printed a simple spur gear mechanism to understand how tolerancing in SolidWorks translates into physical parts. Using a 0.3 mm tolerance, I found that the larger gear fit the base more smoothly than the smaller one, likely because the printer nozzle had to make sharper turns during the smaller gear’s print. After lightly filing the base, the smaller gear achieved the same smooth motion as the larger one.

These early projects have given me valuable insights into both the technical and practical aspects of additive manufacturing, and this section will continue to grow as I print more complex parts and experiment with new ideas.

Wheel Wells & Tie Rod Cover Design - UTSM

Project Details

As part of University of Toronto Supermilage Team's (UTSM) Mechanical team, I designed 2 aerodynamic wheel wells and 2 tie rod covers and integrated them into the full CAD assembly in SolidWorks. I used SolidWorks to measure the available space for these components and accounted for their full range of motion to ensure that the design covered the wheels and tie rods throughout every aspect of a turn. My focus was on meeting regulatory requirements while also maintaining proper clearances and alignment within the larger assembly. This process improved the structural fit of the design and contributed to the overall aerodynamic efficiency of the vehicle. It also gave me valuable experience working with large-scale CAD models and collaborating within a complex design workflow.

Chestnut Residence Green Roof Design

Project Details

The Hanging Gardens of Chestnut was a conceptual green roof design for the 3rd-floor rooftop of Chestnut Residence, a dorm with over 1,100 students. The goal was to make the rooftop a safe, welcoming, and visually appealing space that could be used year-round, while also improving energy efficiency by reducing heat loss. This redesign was projected to save about $12,000 annually on HVAC costs, giving the client a 0.5% to 2% return on investment.

I worked with a team of five engineers, and together we spent countless hours researching, modeling, and evaluating over 50 design concepts using Excel and AutoCAD. After narrowing them down, we developed a high-feasibility green roof plan that balanced both functionality and efficiency.

My role also involved conducting client interviews and technical scoping sessions to make sure we captured the client’s vision. I helped turn those insights into a detailed 70+ page concept design specification that fit within the budget and code requirements. Finally, I presented our proposal, along with a slide deck, to the Engineering Manager and department executives, and our design was approved to move forward into the implementation feasibility study.

This project was completed in: April 2025

Dynamic Systems Vibration Simulator (Python/Matplotlib)

Project Details

The first-year Dynamics course taught me more than just the fundamentals of physics behind moving objects. After learning about moving particles, systems of particles, rigid bodies, and linkages, I found vibrations particularly intriguing. Although this was an exciting new concept, I struggled to visualize the connection between vibrations and differential equations, specifically how vibrations are modeled using them. I realized that my understanding of the material would improve if I had a program where I could manipulate different conditions and variables of a vibration and see both a visual representation of the motion and the differential equation that describes it. After some research, I came across a few resources where others had created similar tools. Inspired by those, I developed an animation using Matplotlib and Python, where I can input a differential equation and see both a vibrating particle on a spring and a simultaneous graph of the equation.

Since this provides a basic understanding of simple vibrations involving only one spring, my next step for the project would be to expand it to more advanced scenarios, such as multiple springs positioned at different angles. Adding this feature, where vibrations are influenced by several sources and factors, would allow users to explore more complex problems and support deeper learning about vibrations and differential equations.

This project was completed in: March 2025
