About Me

I'm a Mechanical Engineering student at the University of Toronto with a strong interest in the intersection of electronics and mechanical systems. My curiosity has led me to explore the fields of mechatronics and robotics, where I’m excited by the possibilities of intelligent design and automation.

As a Mechanical Design Engineer on the U of T Supermileage team, I contribute to the design and manufacturing of a hydrogen-powered, fuel-efficient vehicle. This experience, along with my personal projects, has allowed me to develop skills in CAD—particularly using SolidWorks to visualize and iterate on mechanical concepts.

In my free time, I enjoy creating CAD models of design ideas that interest me, focusing on concepts I hope to bring to life through my 3D Printer. While some of these projects remain digital for now, they reflect my passion for design thinking and engineering creativity.

As I continue to grow and develop as an engineer, I invite you to take a look at some of the work I've done so far and follow along on my journey.

Dynamic Systems Vibration Simulator (Python/Matplotlib)

Show Project Details

The first-year Dynamics course taught me more than just the fundamentals of physics behind moving objects. After learning about moving particles, systems of particles, rigid bodies, and linkages, I found vibrations particularly intriguing. Although this was an exciting new concept, I struggled to visualize the connection between vibrations and differential equations; specifically, how vibrations are modeled using them. I realized that my understanding of the material would improve if I had some kind of calculator or program where I could manipulate different conditions and variables of a vibration and see both a visual representation of the motion and the differential equation that describes it. After some googling and research, I came across a few resources where others had created similar tools. Inspired by those, I developed an animation using Matplotlib and Python, where I can simply input a differential equation and see both a vibrating particle on a spring and a simultaneous graph of the equation.


Since this provides a basic understanding of simple vibrations involving only one spring, my next step for the project would be to expand it to more advanced scenarios, such as multiple springs positioned at different angles. Adding this feature, where vibrations are influenced by several sources and factors, would allow users to explore more complex problems and support deeper learning about vibrations and differential equations.
